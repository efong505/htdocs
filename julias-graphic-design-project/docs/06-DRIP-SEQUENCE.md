## Email #2 — The #1 Reason Your Materials Aren't Working
**Send:** Day 2
**Subject:** The #1 reason your marketing materials aren't working
**Preview text:** It's not what you think...

---

Hey {first_name},

After designing for businesses for over a decade, I see the same mistake everywhere. And it's probably not what you'd expect.

**The #1 problem: Your graphics talk about YOU instead of your CUSTOMER.**

Here's what I mean. Look at most business flyers, brochures, and websites. They say things like:

- "We've been in business since 1998"
- "We offer the best quality"
- "Our team is dedicated to excellence"

That's nice. But your customer is thinking: *"What's in it for ME?"*

**The fix is simple.** Instead of leading with who you are, lead with what your customer gets.

❌ "We offer premium landscaping services"
✅ "Come home to a yard your neighbors envy — without lifting a finger"

❌ "Family-owned bakery since 1985"
✅ "Fresh-baked bread that tastes like your grandmother made it — ready by 7am every morning"

See the difference? The second version makes the customer the hero, not the business.

**This applies to everything** — your business cards, your brochures, your website, your social media. Every piece of marketing should answer one question: *"Why should I care?"*

If you went through the guide, Principle 1 covers this in detail. If you haven't downloaded it yet, here's the link again:
**[Download the Guide]({download_link})**

Next time, I'll share what professional graphic design actually costs in 2026 — real numbers, no dodging.

— Julia

## Email #3 — What Professional Design Actually Costs
**Send:** Day 5
**Subject:** What professional graphic design actually costs in 2026
**Preview text:** Real numbers from a real designer...

---

Hey {first_name},

"How much does a logo cost?" is the question I get asked most. And the honest answer is: it depends. But I'm going to give you real numbers instead of being vague.

**The DIY Route: $0–$50**
- Canva, free logo makers, or your nephew who "knows Photoshop"
- Cost: Almost nothing
- Result: Looks like everyone else's. No ownership of the design files. Often can't be scaled for print without looking blurry.

**The Fiverr/Budget Route: $50–$300**
- Overseas designers working from template libraries
- Cost: Cheap upfront
- Result: You might get something decent. You might get clip art with your name slapped on it. No strategy, no revisions, no relationship.

**The Professional Freelancer Route: $500–$3,000**
- Custom design from an experienced designer (this is where I work)
- Cost: Real investment
- Result: Original hand-crafted design. Multiple concepts. Revisions. All file formats. A design that's actually yours — not a template someone else is also using.

**The Agency Route: $5,000–$50,000+**
- Full brand strategy, market research, focus groups
- Cost: Significant
- Result: Premium product. But most small businesses don't need this level.

**Here's what I tell my clients:**

The question isn't "how much does design cost?" It's "how much is it costing you to look unprofessional?"

A cheap logo that looks generic costs you every customer who glances at your business card and thinks "meh." A professional logo that communicates quality, trust, and personality pays for itself in the first few clients it helps you win.

I work with small businesses to find the right investment for their goals. No overselling — just what you actually need.

## Email #4 — The $200 Logo Cautionary Tale
**Send:** Day 8
**Subject:** How a $200 logo almost cost one business everything
**Preview text:** A true story about cutting corners on design...

---

Hey {first_name},

I want to share a quick story.

A restaurant owner — let's call her Maria — was opening her first location. She had the menu perfected, the space was beautiful, and she was ready to launch. But she needed a logo, menus, and signage fast.

She found someone online who'd do the whole package for $200. Done in 3 days. Seemed like a great deal.

Here's what happened:

1. **The logo was a template.** She found the exact same design being used by a restaurant in another state. Her "custom" logo wasn't custom at all.

2. **The menu was hard to read.** Bad font choices and no visual hierarchy meant customers couldn't find what they wanted. Her servers spent extra time explaining the menu instead of turning tables.

3. **The files were wrong.** When she went to get her sign made, the logo file was too low-resolution. The sign shop charged her $300 to recreate it from scratch.

4. **She had to rebrand within a year.** After realizing her materials looked amateur compared to competitors, she hired a professional designer to start over. Total cost of the "cheap" route: over $2,000 — plus a year of looking unprofessional.

**The lesson isn't "never go cheap."** The lesson is: **understand what you're actually getting.**

When I design a logo, you get:
- Hand-drawn concepts (not templates)
- Multiple directions to choose from
- Revisions until you're happy
- Every file format you'll ever need (print, web, social, signage)
- A design that's legally and creatively yours

That's the difference between a $200 logo and a professional one.

**Did you complete the Brand Identity Checklist from the guide?** If you're missing more than 3 items on that list, your brand has gaps that are costing you customers.

## Email #5 — Canva vs. a Real Designer
**Send:** Day 11
**Subject:** Canva vs. a real designer — an honest comparison
**Preview text:** I'm not going to bash Canva. Here's the truth...

---

Hey {first_name},

I get this question a lot: "Why would I hire a designer when I can use Canva?"

Fair question. And I'm not going to bash Canva — it's a useful tool. But here's an honest comparison so you can decide what's right for your business.

**Canva is great for:**
- Quick social media posts
- Internal documents and presentations
- Event flyers for your kid's school
- Experimenting with ideas before hiring a designer
- Businesses with zero design budget (yet)

**A professional designer is better for:**
- Your logo (Canva logos are templates — thousands of businesses use the same ones)
- Business cards, letterheads, and stationery (brand consistency matters)
- Marketing materials that need to convert (design + strategy)
- Anything that will be printed (Canva's print output is limited)
- Custom illustrations (Canva can't draw)
- Packaging design (this requires technical knowledge of print specs)

**The real difference:**

Canva gives you access to templates. A designer gives you access to *thinking.*

When I design your marketing materials, I'm not just making things look nice. I'm thinking about:
- Who is your customer and what motivates them?
- What do you want them to DO after seeing this?
- How does this piece fit into your overall brand?
- Will this work in print AND digital?
- Is the visual hierarchy guiding the eye to the right place?

Canva can't do that. It's a tool, not a strategist.

**My honest advice:**

Use Canva for the small stuff. Hire a professional for the things that represent your business to the world — your logo, your core marketing materials, and anything that needs to make a first impression.

If you're not sure which category your project falls into, I'm happy to tell you — honestly.

After years of working with business owners, these questions come up in almost every first conversation. Here are my honest answers.

**1. "How long does a project take?"**
A logo: 1-2 weeks. Business card or flyer: 3-5 days. Full brand identity package: 2-4 weeks. The biggest delay is usually waiting on content and feedback from the client — so the faster you respond, the faster you get your finished design.
